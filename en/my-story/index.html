My Story

I don't sell tours. I share what makes me feel alive.

Some mornings I wake up before dawn just to watch the sun rise over Mahébourg. Not because I have a tour. Just because that view reminds me why I'm alive. And when I see my travellers' eyes light up at the same sight...

I know I'm exactly where I'm supposed to be.

Let me tell you

2018 — The turning point

The day I walked away from everything

I was 38. Head concierge at a five-star hotel in Grand Baie. Impeccable uniform. Catalogue smile. "Good morning, how may I help you?" I had the title. The salary. The security.

One evening, a French couple checks in. Tired. Disappointed. With those eyes you get when you've paid a lot for something that wasn't worth the price. "We did all the official tours. Fifty people on a bus. The guide gave us ten minutes at each stop. Beautiful in photos. Empty in our hearts."

I smiled. Professionally. I gave them spa vouchers.

That night, I didn't sleep.

Because I knew. I knew where to find the hidden waterfall where the water is so clear you can see fish five metres down. I knew the Grand Baie fisherman who goes to sea at 5am singing séga while pulling his nets. I knew where to eat the best dholl puri on the island, in a nameless shack where locals queue from dawn.

But I was selling brochures. I was complicit in a lie.

These tourists came searching for the soul of Mauritius. And the tourism industry was selling them an empty shell.

The next morning, I handed in my resignation. No plan B. No safety net. Just a certainty in my gut, as solid as the basalt of Le Morne: if not me, who?

In 2019, Pretty Tours was born. Not an agency. Not a business plan. A promise.

The confession

The real reason I do this

People think I do this job to "share my culture". That's what I say in interviews. It's clean. It's marketable.

The truth is far more selfish than that.

I do this because it saves me.

There are days when I feel invisible in my own country. As if Mauritius has become a backdrop for tourists and I'm just a walk-on role. As if this island I love so fiercely had forgotten the people who carry it in their blood.

Then a WhatsApp message arrives. Sophie, from Lyon. She wants to "see Mauritius differently". We meet at 6am in Mahébourg. I watch her discover the fish market. Fishermen unloading still-trembling tuna. The smell of sea mixed with spices. Creole crackling like jazz.

She cries. Not from sadness. From wonder.

And in that moment, I remember why I'm alive.

Through her eyes, I rediscover my island as if for the first time. Every excursion, I'm the one who's travelling. I'm the one falling in love again with this rock lost in the Indian Ocean.

My travellers are not clients. They are my mirrors. They remind me of the beauty I had stopped seeing.

Unfiltered

What I never say out loud

Social media shows Pretty smiling, energetic, passionate. That's true. But it's not the whole truth.

There are days when I'm afraid. Afraid my travellers won't feel what I feel. Afraid I won't live up to what they hope for. Afraid Mauritius won't touch them the way it touched me.

There are nights when I re-read messages from past travellers to remind myself that what I do matters. That I'm not crazy to stake everything on the strange idea that showing someone a sunrise can change a life.

And then I remember.

I remember the couple who renewed their vows on the beach after an excursion with me.
I remember the teenager battling depression who wrote: "Pretty, you made me want to live."
I remember the businessman who wept at the Seven Coloured Earth — because it was the first time in twenty years he'd felt something.

I don't save lives. But I wake them up.

And maybe that's enough.
